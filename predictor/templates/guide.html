Burn First Aid Guide

Immediate care steps before professional medical treatment, based on clinical and WHO guidelines.

Content aligned with WHO Burn Prevention & Care guidelines
🟢

First-Degree Burn

Superficial

Symptoms

Redness Minor swelling Dry skin Painful to touch No blisters

Immediate First Aid (WHO)

  • 1Cool the burn under cool running water for at least 10–20 minutes
  • 2Remove watches, rings, or tight clothing near the area before swelling begins
  • 3Apply soothing lotion, aloe vera gel, or a moisturiser to relieve discomfort
  • 4Cover loosely with a sterile non-stick gauze bandage or clean cloth
  • 5Take over-the-counter pain relief such as ibuprofen or paracetamol if needed
  • 6Monitor for signs of infection over 24 to 48 hours

Do NOT

  • ✕Apply ice or ice-cold water — causes frostbite and deeper tissue damage
  • ✕Use butter, toothpaste, or oil — traps heat and increases infection risk
  • ✕Break any skin even if it peels — exposes wound to bacteria

Expected Recovery

Usually heals within 3–5 days without scarring. Skin may peel as it heals — this is normal. Seek care if pain worsens after 48 hours.

Usually manageable at home without hospital visit
🟡

Second-Degree Burn

Partial Thickness

Symptoms

Blisters Deep redness Wet / shiny skin Intense pain Swelling Scarring risk

Immediate First Aid (WHO)

  • 1Immediately cool under running water for at least 20 minutes — do not delay
  • 2Remove jewellery and clothing near the burn before swelling occurs, unless stuck to skin
  • 3Do NOT break blisters — they protect the wound from infection
  • 4Cover loosely with sterile non-adhesive dressing or clean cling wrap
  • 5Elevate the burned area above heart level if possible to reduce swelling
  • 6Seek medical care if the burn is larger than 3 centimetres, or on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or a joint
  • 7Do not apply any creams or home remedies before professional assessment

Do NOT

  • ✕Pop or drain blisters — dramatically increases infection and scarring risk
  • ✕Apply adhesive bandages directly on the wound — causes further damage on removal
  • ✕Use fluffy cotton wool — fibres stick to the wound surface

Expected Recovery

Superficial second-degree: 2–3 weeks. Deep second-degree may require skin grafting and can leave permanent scars.

Seek medical care for burns larger than 3 cm or on sensitive areas
🔴

Third-Degree Burn

Full Thickness — Emergency

Symptoms

White / charred skin Leathery texture No pain (nerve damage) No blisters Dry / waxy appearance

Emergency Response (WHO)

  • 1Call emergency services immediately — this is a life-threatening emergency
  • 2Ensure your own safety first — remove the person from danger if safe to do so
  • 3Do NOT apply water, ice, or any cream or ointment to the wound
  • 4Cover loosely with a clean dry cloth or sterile dressing — do not wrap tightly
  • 5Keep the person lying down, warm, and still to prevent shock
  • 6Elevate burned limbs above heart level if possible and if not causing pain
  • 7Monitor breathing — severe burns can cause airway swelling; be ready to give CPR
  • 8Do not give food or drink — surgery may be required immediately upon arrival

Do NOT

  • ✕Remove clothing or material stuck to the wound — causes further trauma and bleeding
  • ✕Apply cold water — can cause hypothermia and shock in large burns
  • ✕Leave the person alone — shock can set in rapidly

Prognosis

Requires hospital admission, likely ICU care, skin grafting, and months of rehabilitation.

Call emergency services immediately — every second counts
Safety

General Safety Guidelines

Never Use Home RemediesButter, oil, toothpaste, egg white, or flour trap heat and bacteria — all significantly worsen burn injuries.

Avoid Ice Directly on SkinIce constricts blood vessels and can cause frostbite on damaged tissue, especially for second and third degree burns.

Remove Tight Items EarlyRemove rings, watches, and tight clothing immediately — swelling occurs rapidly and these can cut off circulation.

Face, Hands & Joints — Seek CareBurns on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or over a joint always require professional medical evaluation regardless of degree.

Watch for Inhalation InjuryBurns near the face or from smoke/chemical exposure may cause airway swelling. Symptoms: hoarseness, difficulty breathing, singed nose hairs.

Hydration MattersSevere burns cause major fluid loss. WHO protocols recommend early intravenous fluid resuscitation for burns covering more than 15% body surface area.

Prevent HypothermiaCooling is essential but prolonged cold water on large burns can cause dangerous drop in body temperature, especially in children and elderly.

Infection PreventionKeep the wound covered and clean. Signs of infection — increasing redness, pus, fever, or odour — require immediate medical attention.

WHO Burn Care Note

According to the World Health Organization, burns are among the most common and devastating forms of trauma. An estimated 180,000 deaths occur annually from burns globally — the vast majority preventable. WHO recommends immediate cooling, wound covering, and rapid transfer to a burn care facility for all moderate-to-severe burns. This guide reflects WHO's published first aid and burn prevention guidelines and is intended for educational purposes only.
